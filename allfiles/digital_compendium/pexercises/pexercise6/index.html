Hyperbolic partial differential equations

Hyperbolic partial differential equations have been used to model wave phenomena occurring in many physical problems, e.g. shallow water waves for Tsunami predictions and blood flow in compliant vessels. For the latter, the governing mass- and momentum equations may be integrated over the vessel cross-section \( A \), yielding: $$ \begin{align} &\frac{\partial A}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = 0 \label{eq:mass}\\ &\frac{\partial Q}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left (\frac{Q^2}{A}\right) = -\frac{A}{\rho}\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} + \frac{A}{\rho} \tau_w \label{eq:mom} \end{align} $$ where \( Q \) denotes the volume flow of the fluid (\( [Q]=\text{m}^3/\text{s} \)), \( \rho \) is fluid density (\( [\rho]=\text{kg}/\text{m}^3 \)), \( p \) is the pressure (\( [p]=\text{Pa}/\text{m}^2 \)), \( \tau_w \) is the wall shear stress, and finally \( x \) and \( t \), refer to space and time, respectively.

The mass- and momentum equations \eqref{eq:mass} and \eqref{eq:mom}, represent an equation system with three unknowns: \( p \), \( A \) and \( Q \), but only with two equations. Thus, to close the system we need an additional state equation (also commonly denoted constitutive equation or material equation) relating pressure and flow. For convenience we choose a simple linear constitutive model as follows, $$ \begin{equation} \label{eq:const} A = A_0 + C\, (p-p_0) \end{equation} $$ where \( A_0 \) and \( p_0 \) refers to a reference area and pressure, respectively, and \( C=\partial A/\partial p \) is the compliance of the blood vessel. By introducing \eqref{eq:const} into \eqref{eq:mass} and \eqref{eq:mom}, the cross-sectional area may be eliminated from the governing equations. Finally, by neglecting the wall shear stress (friction at the walls), the non-linear, inviscid governing equations for blood flow in compliant vessels may be formulated as follows, $$ \begin{align} &\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial }{\partial x} \left ( \frac{Q}{C} \right )= 0 \label{eq:massII}\\ &\frac{\partial Q}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left (\frac{Q^2}{A}\right) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left (\frac{A_0 \, p}{\rho}\right ) \label{eq:momII} \end{align} $$ where \( A \) is given by equation \eqref{eq:const}.

It can be shown that the linearized inviscid forms of \eqref{eq:massII} and \eqref{eq:momII} have solutions of the form: $$ \begin{equation} p = p_0 \, f(x-ct) + p^{'}_0 \, g(x+ct)=p_f + p_b \qquad \text { and } \qquad Q = Q_0 \, f(x-ct) + Q^{'}_0 \, g(x+ct) =Q_f + Q_b \label{} \end{equation} $$ i.e. the pressure \( p \) is composed of two waves \( p_f \) and \( p_b \), respectively, propagating opposite directions with identical wave speed \( c \): $$ \begin{equation} c^2 = \frac{A_0}{\rho C} \label{eq:pwv} \end{equation} $$ When nonlinearities (i.e. the convective terms) are accounted for, solutions on the following form may be found: $$ \begin{equation} p = p_0 \, f(x-c_f t) + p^{'}_0 \, g(x+c_b t)=p_f + p_b \qquad \text { and } \qquad Q = Q_0 \, f(x-c_f t) + Q^{'}_0 \, g(x+c_b t) =Q_f + Q_b \label{} \end{equation} $$ where: $$ \begin{equation} c_f = c + v_0 \qquad \text{and} \qquad c_b = c - v_0, \qquad \text{with} \qquad v_0=\frac{Q_0}{A_0} \label{eq:nonlin_pwv} \end{equation} $$ Use \eqref{eq:nonlin_pwv} to compute the CFL-number for numerical schemes.

It can also be shown that: $$ \begin{equation} \frac{p_0}{Q_0} = \frac{\rho c}{A_0} = Z_c \label{eq:Zc} \end{equation} $$ where \( Z_c \) is called the characteristic impedance \( Z_c \). For a given initial pressure field, use \( Z_c \) to compute a corresponding flow field by Q=p/Z_c$.
