Mathematical pendulum

Figure 1: Mathematical pendulum.

Figure 1 shows a mathematical pendulum where the motion is described by the following ODE: $$ \begin{align} \frac{d ^2\theta}{d\tau^2} + \frac{g}{l} \sin\theta &=0 \label{eq:1} \end{align} $$

with initial conditions $$ \begin{align} &\theta(0) =\theta_0 \\ &\frac{d \theta}{d\tau}(0)=\dot\theta_0 \end{align} $$

We introduce a dimensionless time \( t=\sqrt{\frac{g}{l}}t \) such that \eqref{eq:1} may be written as $$ \begin{align} \ddot\theta(t) + \sin\theta(t) &=0 \label{eq:2} \end{align} $$

with initial conditions $$ \begin{align} &\theta(0) =\theta_0 \\ &\dot\theta(0)=\dot\theta_0 \end{align} $$

Assume that the pendulum wire is a massless rod, such that \( -\pi \leq \theta_0 \leq \pi \).

The total energy (potential + kinetic), which is constant, may be written in dimensionless form as $$ \begin{align} \frac{1}{2}(\dot\theta)^2 - \cos\theta &= \frac{1}{2}(\dot\theta_0)^2 - \cos\theta_0 \label{eq:energy} \end{align} $$ We define an energy function \( E(\theta) \) from \eqref{eq:energy} as $$ \begin{align} E(\theta) &= \frac{1}{2} [(\dot\theta)^2 - (\dot\theta_0)^2] + \cos\theta_0 - \cos\theta \label{eq:energyfunction} \end{align} $$ We see that this function should be identically equal to zero at all times. But, when it is computed numerically, it will deviate from zero due to numerical errors.

Problem 1: Euler's method

Write a Python programme that solves the ODE in \eqref{eq:2} with the specified initial conditions using Euler's method, for given values of \( \theta_0 \) and \( \dot\theta_0 \). Set for instance \( \dot\theta_0=0 \). Experiment with different time steps \( \Delta t \), and carry out the computation for at least a whole period.

Plot both the amplitude and the energy function in \eqref{eq:energyfunction} as functions of \( t \).
